02

Every blocker, resolved to a verdict

Five capabilities are structurally out of Cockpit's reach the incumbent way — no OTA API partnership, no channel-manager API. Each one got checked against current platform/API terms and official connectivity documentation. Three sub-findings came back honestly NOT-VIABLE rather than forcing an answer.

1. Two-way channel sync (pushing rates/availability out)

Not viable to replicate

Chosen pivotRead-only sidecar next to the host's existing publisher — already the architecture. Public precedent exists for analytics products that sit beside the booking platforms rather than replacing a channel manager. No comparable two-way OTA sync, price push, or auto-send without approved platform connectivity. Position Cockpit as read-only plus draft-only assist.

What it costs usiCal gives availability blocking only — one-way, asymmetric, and throttled. Airbnb says imported calendars automatically update every 3 hours and manual refresh is request-limited; Vrbo supports up to five imported calendars per property, mobile-app sync every 30 minutes, and imported events may take up to 20 minutes to appear. Airbnb's current API terms require an organization/API agreement and scoped program access; Vrbo Rapid is limited to select partners and documents its own approved API surface. Without that onboarding, Cockpit has no supported write path for OTA inventory, rates, messaging, or reviews.

Honest framing for hostsCockpit shows the truth; it never becomes a second system that can cause a double-booking — which every incumbent's own users complain about.

2. Payments (taking a cut, processing bookings)

Viable pivot

Chosen pivotFlat SaaS plus Plaid Transactions read-side reconciliation. No Plaid Transfer or OTA payment processing; Stripe Connect only for Cockpit-owned direct bookings.

3. Real-time data without webhooks

Viable, with an honest ceiling

Chosen pivotGmail watch can be a fast mailbox-change trigger, not a webhook payload. Cockpit still fetches/parses messages, and content depends on platform email bodies. For PadSplit Messenger, the inspected emails proved notification/deep-link routing metadata and did not contain the member's message text, so Phase 1 stores the alert and link for human review only. The Airbnb host-side inquiry class inspected in Gmail contained guest-written text; other message classes are outside Phase 1.

4. Writing back — replying to guests, changing prices

Draft-only viable; price write-back not viable

Chosen pivotDraft-only reply assist: generate suggested replies and require the host to review and send inside the platform. No silent auto-send, no automated price changes, and no claim of official platform integration unless separately approved. Airbnb prohibits bots, scrapers, or other automated means unless authorized; no co-host exception was found in the cited language.

5. Screening, smart locks, reviews

Locks & screening viable · reviews not viable

Locks & screeningSmart-lock integrations can be built through independent providers such as Seam and RemoteLock. Current vendor pages also document screening or identity-verification availability across all eight platforms, through native flows, add-ons, or named partners; implementation depth and consent flows still vary by vendor.

ReviewsThe honest failure case. No checked source confirms public Airbnb Reviews API access for non-partners. Do not pitch review automation without approved platform access or a separately verified lawful source.

03

What we're deliberately not building

Naming these explicitly so nobody promises them later by accident — each one was researched, not assumed away.

  • ✕Automated price-push to any OTA without approved platform connectivity — assume unsupported and ToS-sensitive.
  • ✕Silent auto-send of guest replies — draft-only suggestions with host review/send inside the platform.
  • ✕Native review sourcing/automation — no confirmed non-partner access to Airbnb's review data.
  • ✕Becoming a payment processor by accident — Plaid Transactions read-side reconciliation only unless money movement is deliberately scoped.
  • ✕Two-way channel distribution — read-only sidecar to the host's existing publisher, by design.
  • ✕Moving Airbnb/Vrbo bookings off-platform — Cockpit can own direct bookings it sources itself, but must not use Airbnb/Vrbo messages or listings to move bookings off-platform.

The wedge — a PadSplit-native feature spec

Public PadSplit materials confirm room-based hosting, host-visible booking controls, Messenger/maintenance routing metadata, booking-request data availability, member score visibility, limited member balance/upcoming-dues visibility, property ratings, move-out-review direction, and an enhanced earnings CSV summarized by property/month. The live booking-request email verified requester, property, response deadline, auto-approval behavior, and review link; the current host UI showed Request (0), so Phase 1 does not depend on the active request queue. The downloaded earnings CSV verifies property/month financial fields, and the downloaded member ledger verifies member/transaction/lifecycle fields listed in the audit.

01

Property financial roll-up

Verified from the PadSplit earnings CSV for property/month and bank-account reconciliation: gross income, booking fee, service fee, transaction fee, adjustments, and earnings. It does not contain room/member/transaction-date keys, so Phase 1 does not infer room-level P&L from this file.

02

Independent member AR ledger

Verified from the PadSplit member-ledger CSV for date, occupancy window, amount, balance, type, reason, property, move-in/out dates, and member identity. Aging buckets and overdue-status definitions are Cockpit-owned derived logic.

03

Payout reconciliation

Verified from the earnings CSV for property/month, gross income, fees, adjustments, earnings, and bank account. Member-level dated reconciliation uses the separate ledger export; room/member/transaction-date keys are not inferred from the earnings file.

04

Per-room P&L

The verified PadSplit earnings CSV has no room, member, or transaction-date key. Per-room P&L is therefore a Cockpit-owned derived feature requiring explicit expense capture and allocation rules, not a claimed PadSplit source field.

05

Member lifecycle + payment-risk funnel

Booking-request fields and member-ledger lifecycle dates are verified. Gmail also verified PadSplit's host-facing Member Termination Warning and Member Reinstated classes, including the $300 balance threshold, 48-hour post-due-date warning window, property/room/member, and resolution state. Active request-queue UI and universal status-history export are excluded from Phase 1.

06

Turnover-cost tracking

Needs a direct cost-capture mechanism; do not infer costs from platform status labels alone.

07

Segment-aware ingestion

PadSplit path: Chrome Gmail validation confirmed host-side Messenger, booking-request, earnings, member-ledger, and export/report emails. Messenger emails contain sender/subject/member/property/deep-link routing metadata, not the member's message body. Airbnb path: the inspected host-side inquiry contained guest-written text; traveler itinerary, receipt, and payment emails were separately identified and must be excluded. MTR records are bank/Baselane/host-ledger centric, not platform-CSV centric. Keep this separate from PadSplit and OTA ingestion.

08

Portfolio reputation visibility

Supported for member score host visibility, property ratings, and move-out reviews. Portfolio aggregation remains a Cockpit-derived view built from the supported source records; no broader export contract is claimed.

09

Utility cost-per-room allocation

The verified PadSplit email/CSV fields contain no utility-cost field. This is a Cockpit-owned derived feature requiring manual entry or a utility-provider integration.

10

MTR deposit ledger + vacancy forecasting

Proposed Cockpit-owned ledger for direct/MTR records, not a claimed source-system field. Keep it separate from OTA payment rights and off-platform-booking rules.

05

The specialist layer — how we earn credibility as a cockpit, not a toy PMS

Cockpit sits alongside a host's existing setup as a read-side analytics and operations layer — so the features that matter most are the ones that make source-traceable host records more useful without pretending to replace the PMS or channel manager. Phase 1 is source-checked and live-validated as a read-only activity inbox plus portfolio dashboard using verified PadSplit email/CSV fields and filtered Airbnb host-side email classes. Full PadSplit Messenger body sync, universal Airbnb message-body sync, active request-queue UI, OTA write-back, and direct-booking money movement are explicit exclusions.

  • →The verified-data trust argument — platform, export, email, and bank-source traceability is the credibility bar for any read-only analytics layer.
  • →Pacing & forecasting — booking-pace trend lines against prior periods; a table-stakes requirement for a credible analytics layer, not an optional nicety.
  • →Trust accounting — Guesty is the only native source-supported platform in the checked set; Hostaway and Uplisting add marketplace-partner evidence. Keep it as a future differentiator, not a table-stakes parity claim.
  • →An opinionated derived health metric — one number per property/room that rolls up occupancy, arrears, and turnover cost, the kind of judgment call a dashboard of raw numbers doesn't make for the host.
  • →A reconciliation-grade audit trail — every derived number traceable back to its source email/CSV row, which is what makes the arrears-ledger and per-room P&L features actually trustworthy rather than another dashboard to doubt.
  • →PadSplit-native metrics — the ten-item wedge spec above, exposed as first-class views, not bolted onto an STR-shaped data model.
  • →Explicit "not a PMS" positioning — say it outright; this is what makes "read-only" read as a feature instead of an apology.
  • →A disclosed setup posture — avoid vendor-specific setup-fee comparisons unless sourced in the investor deck; say "low-friction onboarding" until pricing evidence is pinned.

07

The operating plan — one queue from signal to proven resolution

The operating unit is an Ops Item, not a message thread. An Ops Item may begin as a guest message, booking request, termination warning, reinstatement, payout, ledger exception, or manual follow-up. Cockpit's job is to identify what needs attention, preserve the source evidence, guide the host to the correct system, and record a truthful resolution.

01

Capture

Ingest only validated email classes, approved exports, iCal availability, extension records, and manual entries.

02

Classify

Separate host-side messages from traveler confirmations, financial events, lifecycle events, and unknown classes.

03

Attach

Link source, account, property, room, contact, deadline, confidence, and evidence ID. Preserve the original deep-link.

04

Prioritize

Rank by external deadline, access/check-in risk, payment exposure, guest impact, and time since arrival.

05

Act

Read, decide, draft, copy, or open the source platform. Keep money movement and platform delivery outside Cockpit.

06

Prove

Resolve only with a source event or explicit host confirmation. Failed ingestion and unconfirmed sends stay visible.

Launch blocker

The current app's “Copy & Mark Sent” action is not truthful enough for production. It can record Sent after copying a draft even though Airbnb or PadSplit has not delivered it. Clipboard failure is also not surfaced. Split this into Copied, Awaiting host confirmation, and Confirmed sent; only the last state may close the response item.

Daily operating surface

  • Now: one ranked queue for messages, booking deadlines, payment risk, access issues, and reconciliation exceptions.
  • Proof: source record, body-availability status, confidence, evidence ID, failed ingestion, and unconfirmed send attempts.
  • Archive: resolved history with owner, timestamps, action, and resolution evidence.
  • Layout: queue on the left, source/thread in the center, action and proof on the right.

Proposed service policy

  • Access/check-in issue: acknowledge within 15 minutes; human escalation within 30.
  • Booking request: triage within 1 hour; reminders six and two hours before its external deadline.
  • Normal guest/member message: triage within 4 hours.
  • Payment risk: review within 2 hours; same-day follow-up when action is required.
  • Payout/reconciliation exception: next-business-day review.

These are proposed operating targets, not competitor claims or externally verified benchmarks.

Operating rhythm: clear or assign the Now queue daily; review blocked sources, parser exceptions, and unconfirmed sends weekly; review statements, portfolio performance, and reconciliation quality monthly. The queue owner is accountable for triage, while the assigned host/operator is accountable for source-platform action and confirmation.

The eight checked competitors operate primarily as systems of record: they centralize reservations and conversations, automate recurring messages, synchronize channels, and increasingly own direct bookings, payments, tasks, and owner reporting. Cockpit should enter as a complementary operations sidecar for fragmented PadSplit + STR/MTR portfolios, not as a smaller imitation of a mature PMS.

Competitive advantages

  • PadSplit and rent-by-room are first-class: room, member, arrears, termination, reinstatement, and turnover are modeled as core operations.
  • One exception queue: messages, deadlines, payment risk, and reconciliation sit in the same ranked work surface.
  • Evidence-grade truth: every claim and resolution carries source, confidence, timestamp, and evidence ID.
  • No forced migration: the host can keep existing PMS/channel systems while Cockpit organizes cross-system work.
  • Lower action risk: read-side operation avoids accidental price pushes, double-booking writes, and off-platform booking movement.
  • Distinct wedge: member lifecycle and room-level economics are not the center of the checked STR products.

Competitive disadvantages

  • No native send or write-back: hosts must switch to the source platform and confirm the outcome.
  • Incomplete bodies: PadSplit Messenger email cannot support reply drafting when the member text is absent.
  • Latency and fragility: email, export, and extension signals can lag or change format.
  • External dependency: source-platform access, permissions, and availability remain outside Cockpit's control.
  • Less ecosystem depth: competitors have mature integrations, brands, support, and operating history.
  • Proof adds friction: Cockpit cannot truthfully claim delivery or resolution without source evidence or host confirmation.
Positioning

“The operations cockpit for fragmented rental portfolios.” Do not sell “a cheaper PMS” or “all-in-one property management.” Lead with PadSplit/rent-by-room depth, a cross-system exception queue, and an evidence trail that tells the operator what needs action now and what was actually resolved.
