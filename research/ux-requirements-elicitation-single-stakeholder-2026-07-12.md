# UX / product-discovery best practices for eliciting requirements and visual preferences from a single stakeholder

**Context:** This research supports an AI coding/design assistant working with one solo builder (Nnamdi) across many iterative sessions on a control-plane dashboard and a "review mode" UI. Goal: get better at eliciting his *real* preferences and requirements so the product is one he loves, not just one that satisfies a literal spec.

**Summary:** The literature on eliciting requirements from a single stakeholder converges on one core move across every discipline surveyed — stop asking people what they want and start reconstructing what they actually did, felt, and struggled with in real past moments, then let them *react* to concrete artifacts (mockups, mood boards, paired comparisons) rather than *describe* abstract preferences in words. Jobs-to-be-Done "switch interviews" (Bob Moesta) and The Mom Test (Rob Fitzpatrick) both operationalize this by banning hypothetical and opinion-seeking questions in favor of specific-past-event reconstruction. Contextual inquiry generalizes it to real-time observation ("watch and ask why, right now, not from memory"). For visual/UI taste specifically, the same "show, don't ask" logic holds: mood boards, paired comparisons, and rapid low-fidelity prototypes work because people can react to a concrete stimulus far more reliably than they can introspect and describe a taste they haven't consciously modeled — aesthetic judgments form in as little as 50 milliseconds and are largely stable after that (Nielsen Norman Group, citing Lindgaard et al.). AI-specific research on this problem names it the "articulation barrier" (Jakob Nielsen) — many people, including skilled ones, cannot reliably type what they want, so the AI's job is to close that gap with visual options, memory of past reactions, and multimodal input rather than by demanding better prompts. A 2025 academic study of professional designers imagining agentic-AI collaboration (Imagining Design Workflows in Agentic AI Futures, OZCHI '25) found designers explicitly want AI to learn their style over time from annotations/sketches/reactions rather than from one-shot text prompts, and explicitly fear AI that executes without checking in or that narrows the idea space by anchoring on its own first suggestion. The clearest, most consistently documented failure modes across all these sources are: leading/closed questions, asking hypothetical "would you use X" questions instead of observing reactions to real past behavior or real artifacts, confirmation bias (mining every answer to support the option already favored), and anchoring — letting the first concept/option shown become the fixed reference point everything else is unfavorably compared against. Below are numbered findings with sources actually opened and read, followed by 8 concrete techniques synthesized for this specific working relationship.

## Findings

### 1. Jobs-to-be-Done "switch interviews" reconstruct real decision moments instead of asking for opinions

Bob Moesta's switch-interview method (documented at jobstobedone.org, the site run by the Rewired Group team that built the technique) works by getting the subject to relive the literal timeline around a real past decision — not by asking what they think about a product. The interviewer asks granular, seemingly-irrelevant questions ("When did you buy this? What day of the week? Who was with you? What else did you purchase? What were you thinking at different points?") to trigger accurate episodic memory rather than post-hoc rationalization. The site is explicit about rejecting theorizing: when a subject starts speculating about alternatives, interviewers redirect with "We want stories of what actually happened, not rationalizations after the fact." The method also uses a "game on / game off" device (phone screen up/down) to separate live interviewing from teaching moments, keeping the technique itself from contaminating the subject's narrative.
**Source:** [jobstobedone.org — The Mattress Interview](https://jobstobedone.org/radio/the-mattress-interview-part-one/) (fetched and read in full)

### 2. The Mom Test: three rules that generalize directly to a single stakeholder

Rob Fitzpatrick's framework (summarized reliably at sachinrekhi.com, a first-hand practitioner writeup) reduces to three rules: (1) talk about their life/reality, not your idea — ask about goals and current behavior, not "would you use my app?"; (2) ask about specifics in the past, not generic opinions about the future — "walk me through when this last happened" beats "would you buy X?"; (3) talk less, listen more. The mechanism cited for rule 1 is what Fitzpatrick calls the "pathos problem" — once you expose your own idea, the other person feels social pressure to be polite, so their answer stops being informative. Concrete question swaps given: "Would you buy this?" → "Tell me about the last time you faced this problem. What did you do?"; "Do you think this is a good idea?" → "Why do you bother doing this at all?" A third element — measuring real commitment (time, reputation, or money spent) rather than verbal enthusiasm — is used to separate polite validation from genuine signal.
**Source:** [sachinrekhi.com — A Primer on Talking to Customers From Rob Fitzpatrick's The Mom Test](https://www.sachinrekhi.com/p/the-mom-test-rob-fitzpatrick) (fetched and read in full). Note: the book's own landing page (momtestbook.com) was also opened but does not itself contain the methodology — it is a sales page, so the rekhi.com summary and cross-corroborating search-result snippets are the actual source of the rule text above.

### 3. Contextual inquiry: watch-and-ask-in-the-moment beats recall because people can explain what they're doing while doing it but not from memory

Nielsen Norman Group's definitive article describes contextual inquiry as combining "Context" (research in the user's actual environment, not a lab) with "Inquiry" (asking questions in real time). It rests on an "apprenticeship model" with four principles: Context, Partnership (researcher and subject as collaborators, not interviewer/subject), Interpretation (shared, checked understanding built live), and Focus (a clear goal guiding what gets observed). The key claim, directly quoted from NN/g: traditional interviews fail because people struggle to accurately recall processes from memory, but "users can easily talk about what they are doing and why when they are doing it." NN/g gives a concrete example: auto-insurance specialists who habitually hit "save" despite autosave being on — a behavior that reveals distrust in the system, which the specialists would likely never have surfaced if simply asked "do you trust the autosave?" The one-on-one session structure is four parts: Primer (rapport/expectations) → Transition (explicit shift into observation mode) → Contextual Interview (watch, then pause to discuss and validate interpretations) → Wrapup (confirm findings).
**Source:** [NN/g — Contextual Inquiry: Inspire Design by Observing and Interviewing Users in Their Context](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/contextual-inquiry/) (fetched and read in full)

### 4. Continuous discovery habits (Teresa Torres): weekly, small, direct touchpoints — not periodic big research projects

Torres's definition, per Product Talk's own glossary: continuous discovery is "weekly touch points with customers by the team building the product, where they conduct small research activities" in pursuit of a specific desired outcome. Three conditions must hold for discovery to count as "continuous": (1) the people building the product interact directly with the customer — not filtered through research reports or personas; (2) research activities are small enough to fit alongside normal build work; (3) the focus stays on outcomes, not on research for its own sake. I attempted to verify whether Torres's own materials address the single-customer case explicitly (relevant here since there is exactly one stakeholder) — the specific page fetched did not address this scenario, so that extension is **not independently sourced**; it is my own reasonable inference that weekly small touchpoints generalize to a single relationship, not a documented Torres claim.
**Source:** [Product Talk — Continuous Discovery glossary entry](https://www.producttalk.org/glossary-discovery-continuous-discovery/) (fetched and read; single-stakeholder extension explicitly flagged as unverified in the page content)

### 5. Attitudinal (self-reported) data and behavioral (observed) data routinely diverge, and the gap itself is where the real insight lives

NN/g's article on attitudinal vs. behavioral research states the divergence plainly: "what users say (attitudinal data) and what they do (behavioral data) are often quite different," and importantly, "the mismatches between user attitudes and user actions are often a great source of insights" — i.e., the goal isn't to eliminate the gap but to notice it. Causes cited: imperfect memory, social-desirability bias (adjusting answers to match perceived norms), and a genuine inability to articulate internal experience in words. NN/g's guidance on which to use: attitudinal methods explain *why* someone perceives something a certain way; behavioral methods predict what someone will actually do. Most real UX research blends both.
**Source:** [NN/g — Attitudinal vs. Behavioral UX Research](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/attitudinal-behavioral/) (fetched and read in full)

### 6. Aesthetic first impressions form in as little as 50 milliseconds and are largely stable afterward

NN/g's article on first impressions cites Gitte Lindgaard et al.'s research finding "a decision on aesthetics is made as early as 50 milliseconds into visiting a site, and rarely changes if you give people more time." The practical implication NN/g draws: since the aesthetic judgment locks in almost immediately, testing methodology should use brief, timed exposures (screen-timeout tests) to capture the *genuine* gut reaction rather than a reasoned, deliberated opinion formed after staring at a mockup for minutes — the deliberated opinion is a different, less reliable signal than the instant one. This directly supports "show a design and read the immediate reaction" over "show a design and ask for a considered critique."
**Source:** [NN/g — First Impressions Matter: How Designers Can Support Automatic Cognitive Processing](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/first-impressions-human-automaticity/) (fetched and read in full; original Lindgaard et al. research is NN/g's own cited source, not independently re-verified against the primary paper — flagged as a secondary citation)

### 7. Preference/paired-comparison testing for visual design: keep it to 2–3 options, always ask "why," and don't mistake "prefers" for "can use"

Maze's preference-testing guide, read in full, gives concrete methodology: cap variants at two to three ("when it gets more than these, it becomes difficult for people to make a comparison"), aim for at least ~20 participants to see a pattern (30+ for more statistical confidence — this doesn't directly transfer to an n=1 stakeholder context, but the *method* of pairing a forced choice with an open "why" does), and always pair the forced-choice question with 2–3 follow-ups plus at least one open-ended "why" question. The explicit, named pitfall: "people often choose what looks cleaner or more modern, even if it is not the clearest or most functional" — preference ≠ usability, so preference tests should be paired with (not substituted for) actual usability/behavioral testing. Mitigation given for order effects: randomize presentation order to reduce primacy/anchoring.
**Source:** [Maze — Preference testing: Choosing the design users love](https://maze.co/blog/preference-testing/) (fetched and read in full)

### 8. Mood boards work because people can react to a concrete visual stimulus more reliably than they can verbally generate an abstract preference — and this is empirically testable, not just folklore

The Interaction Design Foundation's mood-board article, read in full, makes the mechanism explicit: mood boards let designers "visually communicate the feelings or values that a team aims to evoke" without requiring the other party to have technical/design vocabulary; during elicitation, "users react to visual stimuli, providing insights into their preferences and emotional responses" that don't surface from direct questioning, because people often recognize what they like before they can explain *why* in words. The IxDF page cites a real, independently-verifiable academic source for this: Chang, H.M., Díaz, M., Català, A., Chen, W., & Rauterberg, M. (2014), "Mood Boards as a Universal Tool for Investigating Emotional Experience," in *Design, User Experience, and Usability* (Springer), pp. 220–231 — I confirmed this citation exists (Springer/ResearchGate/Academia.edu all list the same paper, same authors, same year) rather than being a fabricated reference. The paper's finding, per IxDF's summary: mood boards have high inter-rater reliability, meaning different people (including non-designers) interpret the same mood board's emotional intent consistently — evidence that a mood board is transmitting real, shared signal about taste, not just noise.
**Sources:** [IxDF — What are Mood Boards in UX Design?](https://ixdf.org/literature/topics/mood-boards) (fetched and read in full); citation existence cross-checked via [ResearchGate listing](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/265383538_Mood_Boards_as_a_Universal_Tool_for_Investigating_Emotional_Experience) and [Springer listing](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-319-07638-6_22) (search-result snippets confirming existence/metadata, not full-text fetched — flagged as metadata-level verification only)

### 9. Kano model: paired functional/dysfunctional questions sort features into must-have vs. delighter vs. indifferent, and it scales down to a single stakeholder

ProductSchool's article, read in full, explains the actual survey mechanic: for each candidate feature, ask two questions — "How would you feel if the product had this feature?" (functional) and "How would you feel if the product did NOT have this feature?" (dysfunctional) — each answered on a 5-point scale (like / expect / neutral / can tolerate / dislike). Cross-referencing the two answers on a standard evaluation table classifies the feature as Must-be, Performance (one-dimensional), Attractive (delighter), Indifferent, or Reverse. ProductSchool's article states directly that this scales to one person: "the approach can apply to single users or stakeholders... this lightweight approach proves useful for quick stakeholder alignment without running formal surveys." A separately-sourced search summary (thinkinsights/justinmind, not independently fetched — flagged) adds that category membership is not static: two-factor authentication moved from delighter → performance → must-have over time as the market matured, so the same feature can and should be re-sorted periodically, not classified once.
**Source:** [ProductSchool — The Kano Model: Prioritizing Features That Delight](https://productschool.com/blog/product-fundamentals/kano-model) (fetched and read in full)

### 10. AI-specific: Jakob Nielsen's "articulation barrier" names the exact problem this assistant is trying to solve

Nielsen's article, read in full, states the core problem in AI UX terms: many users — not just low-literacy ones, though he notes roughly half the population in literacy-stratified countries struggles with descriptive writing — cannot reliably generate a text description of what they want, especially compared to *recognizing* it once shown. He calls the existence of "prompt engineering" as advice itself "a fundamental design failure": users shouldn't need special technique to be understood. His recommended design patterns, directly applicable to a design-assistant AI: (1) **Prompt Augmentation** — offer galleries/visual style options so the user can recognize rather than describe; (2) **Aided Prompt Understanding** — help refine vague intent through guided back-and-forth rather than a single blind attempt; (3) **Visible User Models** — persist the user's known preferences/constraints/communication style across sessions so they never have to re-explain; (4) **Multimodal Intent Capture** — accept sketches, images, voice, not just text, since verbal articulation is the bottleneck, not the actual taste; (5) **Implicit Intent Inference** — use ambient signals (patterns, hesitations, history) to draft a likely intent for confirmation rather than always demanding the user type first.
**Source:** [Jakob Nielsen — Intent by Discovery: Designing the AI User Experience](https://jakobnielsenphd.substack.com/p/intent-ux) (fetched and read in full)

### 11. A 2025 academic study of professional designers imagining agentic-AI collaboration: designers want the AI to learn from reactions over time, and explicitly fear anchoring/over-autonomy

"Imagining Design Workflows in Agentic AI Futures" (OZCHI '25, published on arXiv), read in full via the HTML version, is a design-fiction study where ten professional designers discussed collaborating with a hypothetical agentic AI. Concrete findings directly relevant here: designers wanted the AI to "record and eventually remember the things that you're doing now" to inform later idea generation — i.e., learn taste from a running history of reactions, not a one-shot brief. They proposed multimodal preference-expression channels beyond text: annotating/tagging specific regions of an artifact, sketching as training input, and voice notes with auto-transcription. One participant noted that scaffolded, pre-filled prompts ("here's an idea that flows out") helped ideation more than a blank text box — i.e., giving the human something concrete to react to and edit beats asking them to generate from nothing. On failure modes, the study surfaced explicit fears: **over-autonomy** (AI executing without the designer's ongoing control), **loss of nuance** (AI can't capture "the diversity of ideas and what each meant" or the "soft conversations" behind creative decisions), and **design fixation** — exposure to the AI's own stereotypical generated outputs narrowing rather than expanding the space of ideas considered (a direct, sourced instance of anchoring-on-the-first-idea-shown, but originating from the AI's own suggestion rather than a human's).
**Source:** [arXiv:2509.20731 — Imagining Design Workflows in Agentic AI Futures](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.20731v1) (fetched and read in full, HTML full-text version)

### 13. Anchoring bias in design specifically: the first concept shown becomes the fixed reference point

Multiple design-bias overview sources (search-snippet level, cross-corroborating but no single one independently fetched in full — flagged) converge on the same mechanism: "the first design concept presented becomes the benchmark, and every other option gets compared to that initial version rather than being evaluated on its own merits," to the point that a genuinely better later option can be dismissed or pressured to conform to the first one simply because it looks different. The same sources note anchoring isn't only about concept order — it also shows up in things like which price tier is shown first on a pricing screen. Named mitigation: randomize/vary presentation order, and deliberately show genuinely divergent (not minor-variation) options so there isn't a single obvious "default" to anchor on.
**Sources (search-snippet corroboration, not independently fetched in full):** results returned by query "anchoring bias first design concept shown influences client feedback design research" — flagged as lower-confidence than findings 1–11 above.

### 14. "Rule of three": presenting divergent options is a documented industry practice, but not an uncontested one

A design-agency practice, described across several design-business sources (search-snippet level), is to present three deliberately divergent concepts: one "safe" option resembling proven prior work, one "adventurous" option that pushes the stakeholder outside their comfort zone, and one "middle ground" blending elements of both. However, the same search surfaced credible dissent: some practitioners argue that more than three options causes decision paralysis (a claim echoed independently in the design-bias literature above), and others argue that with a well-understood stakeholder, presenting a single, well-reasoned option is sufficient and avoids diluting conviction. The clear, corroborated failure mode named across sources: a "three-option" review is often actually one real option with two token variations, "which looks lazy and doesn't necessarily help the client" — divergence has to be real, not cosmetic. **Not independently fetched in full** — treat as directionally reliable but lower-confidence than the fully-read sources above.
**Sources (search-snippet level):** results returned by query "how many design concepts to show client rule of three divergent options before converging," including Design Shack and a Google Design "Rule of Three" reference.

## Concrete techniques for this team

Synthesized from the findings above, specifically for how this assistant should work with Nnamdi across iterative design/build sessions on the control-plane dashboard and review-mode UI:

1. **Never ask "would you like X" or "does this work for you" as the primary probe — ask what he actually did last time he hit this problem.** Per findings 1–2 (JTBD, Mom Test), hypothetical and opinion questions get polite, low-signal answers. Before proposing a new flow or screen, ask about the last real instance of the underlying task ("walk me through the last time you had to check on a run" / "what did you actually do the last time this broke") rather than "would a dashboard like this help?"

2. **Show, then watch, before asking for a verbal critique.** Per findings 5 and 6, stated opinions and observed reactions diverge, and aesthetic judgment is fast and largely locked-in within moments. When presenting a mockup or UI direction, get the immediate gut reaction first (what does he click, what does he ignore, what's his first unprompted comment) before opening it up to deliberate discussion — the deliberate discussion is a real but *different* signal, not a purer version of the same one.

3. **Default to 2–3 genuinely divergent options, not one polished answer, when the direction is actually unclear.** Per findings 7 and 14, paired/multi-option comparison surfaces real preference and reduces anchoring, but only if the options are substantively different (not one real option plus cosmetic variants) — and per finding 13, whichever option is shown first will get anchored on, so vary the order and don't always lead with the assistant's own favorite.

4. **Treat every one of Nnamdi's stated feature requests as a Kano-style pair, not a single yes/no.** Per finding 9, ask (explicitly or implicitly, by watching his reaction) both "how would this feel if present" and "how would it feel if absent" — this distinguishes a must-have (breaks trust if missing) from a delighter (nice surprise, not required) from something he's actually indifferent to and shouldn't be built at all. Re-sort periodically; something that was a delighter in an earlier session (e.g., a novel visualization) can become a baseline expectation later.

5. **Build a persistent, visible model of his taste instead of re-eliciting from scratch each session.** Per finding 10 (Nielsen's "Visible User Models") and finding 11 (designers wanting the AI to "remember the things you're doing now" for later idea generation), track concrete, reusable signal — colors/layouts/patterns he's reacted well or badly to, phrases he uses to describe what he likes, prior direction choices — and reference it explicitly ("last time you preferred the denser layout over the spacious one, so I leaned that way here") rather than starting blank each time.

6. **Narrate the tradeoff behind every option, not just the option itself.** This is a direct synthesis, not independently sourced as a named technique, but follows from finding 11's fear of "loss of nuance" (AI can't capture the reasoning behind a choice) and finding 3's "Interpretation" principle (shared, checked understanding built live, not assumed). When presenting a design choice, state what was optimized for and what was given up, so agreement or disagreement is informed rather than reflexive.

7. **Prefer multimodal, low-friction preference capture over asking him to describe taste in words.** Per findings 8 (mood boards) and 10 (multimodal intent capture) and 11 (annotation/sketch/voice over blank-prompt text), when taste is ambiguous, offer something to react to — a small mood-board-style set of visual references, a rough low-fidelity sketch, or a quick paired comparison — rather than asking "what style do you want?" His reaction to a concrete stimulus is more reliable data than his verbal self-report.

8. **Watch for AI-side anchoring, not just human-side anchoring.** Finding 11's "design fixation" result is the most AI-specific failure mode found: the assistant's own first generated suggestion can narrow the space of what gets considered, including in the assistant's own follow-up generations. When iterating, deliberately generate from a genuinely different starting point sometimes rather than only refining the first draft, and flag explicitly when a new idea is a variation vs. a real alternative.
